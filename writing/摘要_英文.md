# ABSTRACT

Accurate dynamic measurement of electron density is essential for understanding electromagnetic propagation in plasma sheaths and for blackout mitigation in hypersonic flight and atmospheric reentry. Conventional microwave interferometry uses phase as the observation quantity and therefore suffers from phase ambiguity in high-density plasma. Linear frequency-modulated continuous-wave (LFMCW) systems mix the transmitted chirp with its echo to produce a beat signal whose frequency is linearly proportional to the propagation delay, thereby circumventing phase ambiguity. However, this approach still fails under strong dispersion, where the frequency dependence of group delay causes beat-spectrum defocusing, peak bias, and breakdown of linear inversion. Instead of treating dispersion as interference to be suppressed, this thesis uses the group-delay trajectory embedded in the beat signal as a new observable and studies LFMCW propagation in dispersive plasma, group-delay trajectory extraction, reduced-order parameter inversion, and engineering validation.

Based on the Drude model, the complex permittivity, propagation constant, and group delay of unmagnetized plasma are derived, and a full-band nonlinear mapping between probing frequency and group delay is established. A group-delay nonlinearity factor is introduced, and an engineering criterion based on local linearization, $\chi(f_c)=B\cdot \eta(f_c)\cdot \tau_0 \lesssim 1$, is established to delineate the local applicability boundary of the conventional full-band FFT method, in which the beat signal is processed by a single FFT and the propagation delay is estimated from the spectral main-peak shift. Theoretical analysis shows that the effect of collision frequency $\nu_e$ on group delay is only a second-order perturbation of order $(\nu_e/\omega)^2$, whereas electron density $n_e$ governs the dominant shape of the delay curve. Accordingly, a reduced-dimensional strategy is adopted by fixing $\nu_e$ and inverting only $n_e$. On this basis, a processing chain of sliding-window analysis, MDL-based source number estimation, TLS-ESPRIT feature extraction, and Metropolis-Hastings MCMC inversion is constructed to reconstruct the frequency-delay trajectory and quantify inversion uncertainty through an amplitude-weighted likelihood function.

For theoretical and simulation-based validation, a dual framework combining MATLAB analytical calculation and CST full-wave simulation is established. Simulations based on the Drude plasma model indicate that, under strong dispersion, the conventional inversion method based on a single FFT peak over the entire beat signal can produce errors exceeding 100%, while the proposed method keeps the inversion error below 0.5% at an SNR of 20 dB and remains stable from 10 dB to 30 dB. Posterior analysis further confirms that $n_e$ is strongly observable, whereas $\nu_e$ is only weakly observable.

For engineering validation using an equivalent medium, a Ka-band wideband LFMCW diagnostic system is developed, and the sweep bandwidth is increased from 800 MHz to 3 GHz. Moving-target calibration demonstrates a minimum delay resolution of 3.33 ps, corresponding to a lower detectable electron density of approximately $1.24 \times 10^{17}$ m$^{-3}$ for a 32 GHz probing center frequency and a 200 mm plasma diameter. A microwave bandpass filter is further employed as a controllable equivalent medium. ADS full-link simulation is used for de-embedding, trajectory extraction, and fitting verification, while hardware measurements with the physical filter further confirm the stable recovery of group-delay trajectory features against S-parameter ground truth. For media with a known mathematical dispersion function, these features can serve as observation inputs for subsequent parameter inversion. These results collectively demonstrate the feasibility of LFMCW group-delay trajectory analysis as a viable diagnostic approach for strongly dispersive plasma.

**Key words:** LFMCW; plasma diagnostics; group-delay trajectory in dispersive media; time-frequency analysis; parameter inversion
